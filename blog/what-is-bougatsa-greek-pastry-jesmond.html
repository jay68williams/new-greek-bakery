What is bougatsa, and why has nobody in Newcastle been making it?

If you have spent any time in Greece, you already know bougatsa. It is the thing you eat first thing in the morning from a bakery counter, still warm, dusted with icing sugar and cinnamon, with layers of crisp handmade phyllo wrapped around a filling of warm semolina custard cream.

It is one of the most popular pastries in the country. Walk into any bakery in Thessaloniki, Athens or Crete before 9am and you will see trays of it coming out of the oven. People queue for it. They eat it standing up. It is not a dessert. It is breakfast.

How bougatsa is made

The pastry starts with phyllo dough, rolled out thin by hand. Not the dry, packaged filo you find in supermarkets. Proper phyllo, stretched until you can almost see through it, brushed with butter, and layered to create that shattering, flaky texture when baked.

The filling is a semolina custard, made with milk, sugar, eggs and fine semolina flour. It is cooked slowly until thick, then spread across the phyllo, folded, and baked until the outside is deep golden and the inside is still soft.

When it comes out of the oven, it is cut into portions, dusted with powdered sugar and a generous amount of cinnamon, and served warm. That contrast between the crisp pastry and the soft, creamy filling is what makes it.

Savoury bougatsa too

In northern Greece, bougatsa also comes in savoury versions. The most common is filled with a mixture of feta cheese, or with seasoned minced meat. Same handmade phyllo, same technique, completely different experience.

The cheese version is simple and sharp. The meat version is richer, almost like a Greek take on a sausage roll but with layers of flaky pastry instead of shortcrust. Both are eaten the same way: hot, from the counter, with a coffee.

Why it does not exist in Newcastle

Bougatsa requires handmade phyllo. It requires someone who knows how to stretch and layer the dough properly. It requires the custard to be made fresh. It is not something that scales easily or travels well from a central kitchen.

That is why you do not see it in Newcastle. There has not been a dedicated Greek bakery with the skills, the equipment, or the commitment to make it properly every morning.

That is changing

New Greek Bakery is opening on Clayton Road in Jesmond. It is a dedicated Greek bakery cafe from the team behind New Greek on Chillingham Road in Heaton.

Bougatsa will be one of the signature items. Handmade phyllo. Fresh custard cream. Baked every morning and served warm. The way it is meant to be eaten.

Alongside bougatsa, the bakery will serve spanakopita, baklava, freshly baked Greek breads, freddo espresso and proper Greek coffee. A full Greek bakery experience, the first of its kind in Newcastle.
